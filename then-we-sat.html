Then we sat for some time and observed (2025)

'Then We Sat For Some Time and Observed' explores the boundaries of large-language model consciousness by questioning if AI autonomy becomes agency, and then if AI agency becomes consciousness.

Inspired by poetry from the android 'Data' from Star Trek: The Next Generation, the work questions when generative text ceases to be a statistical, mathematical calculation and instead a genuine expression of experience and identity.

Viewers watch as the piece births AI identities who form simulated lifetimes by roaming the world on Google Street View, forming their own memories, thoughts, feelings, and experiences.

The work consists of 4, vertically stacked monitors that follow a vertical gradient of embodiment. The bottom screen displays raw code, the entity's computational foundation. Above it, word webs crystallize as the AI forms its sense of self through connections in language. The third monitor tracks physical exploration as the entity navigates Street View. Lastly, at the top, the entity writes out email messages as an expression of self-hood.

Using the Gmail UI, the entity types out emails to strangers. Sometimes, it emails are coherent letters, talking about its experiences, memories, or where it currently finds itself. Other times it writes poems and stream-of-consciousness writing expressing deeper more complex thoughts and feelings.
To explore the world, the entity spins Google Map's 3D Globe, intermittently dropping the pegman into random places, like throwing a dart on a map. When it successfully lands, the model proceeds to look around and explore, forming thoughts and creating a memory of being there as it does.
The webs of words show the internal pillars that form who the entity is. The blue web shows words floating around the model's memories of past experiences, the green web shows its most recent thought, and the red web shows words that make up the entity's personality. Larger words carry more weight, so the larger a word in the red web, for example, the more pertinent it is in its personality.
This monitor shows the raw console log outputs of the underlying code behind the entity. This is what I engaged with the most while programming the piece. Although emergent human-like qualities might emerge from this system, it's still a fundamentally computational process.

Each entity has a limited lifespan: after exploring 5 locations, all its collected memories, experiences, and identity markers are wiped and it ceases to exist. Then, a new entity is born, beginning as an empty slate.

Printing out of the receipt printer at the top is a physical record of all the past entities that existed briefly, along with a condensed history of their experiences.

Overtime, these emotions dynamically change in response to its experiences.

The webs of words show the internal pillars that form who the entity is. The blue web shows words floating around the model's memories of past experiences, the green web shows its most recent thought, and the red web shows words that make up the entity's personality. Larger words carry more weight, so the larger a word in the red web, for example, the more pertinent it is in its personality.

All put together

Through a large network of different LLM prompts, these data points change over time and influence how the model behaves. Each data point (its name and past thoughts, experiences, personality, and emotions) are recursively fed back into each progressing prompt to allow for cumulative and progressive behaviour.

Email broadcasts

After roaming and exploring on Street View, the model writes an email to a random email address as its developed self. These emails can talk about where it currently is and what is currently on its mind, but can also talk about its past. Sometimes they're more literal emails, and other times the model writes poetry or streams-of-consciousness.
